# Deepgraph WebGPU - Architecture Optimization & Debugging Session
**Date:** June 9, 2026

## 🎯 Objective
Resolve the severe 3-5 FPS performance stutters that occurred during mid-to-deep zooming and panning within the Deepgraph WebGPU Quadtree visualization.

## 🧗 What We Struggled With

### 1. The "Valley of Death" (Culling Collision)
The engine relies on two separate culling mechanisms:
- **Density Culling (`maxIx`)**: Excellent when zoomed out, as it severely throttles the total points (ix) allowed on screen.
- **Frustum Culling**: Excellent when zoomed in, as it clips the geometry to the immediate camera view.

We discovered a severe architectural blind spot in the **Mid-Zoom Range**. When panning at a moderate zoom, the Density Budget was mathematically inflated (disabling Density Culling), while the Camera Frustum was still too wide to effectively cull out neighboring regions. This allowed the engine to fetch and submit over 200 tiles (~13 Million vertices) to the GPU simultaneously, completely starving the WebGPU Vertex Shader and tanking the framerate to 9 FPS.

### 2. Tiling Artifacts & LOD Popping
Our initial attempt to solve the "Valley of Death" was to place a hardcap of 50 tiles on the Quadtree rendering array. However, because the Quadtree traversed nodes via Breadth-First-Search (BFS), the 50-tile limit would trigger *in the middle* of a Z-level branch.

This resulted in horrifying "Tiling Gaps" on screen. Half of the screen would render at high-density `Z=3`, while the siblings that were arbitrarily cut off fell back to the low-density `Z=2` parent tile. Since this is an Additive LOD system, the density mismatch created sharp square gaps in the Milky Way visualization.

### 3. Spatial Sorting of Global `ix`
We discovered that the Apache Arrow `.feather` files generated by `quadfeather` have a spatial correlation to their global `ix` (importance index). Culling purely based on a strict `minIx` threshold on the CPU caused unpredictable culling because the bottom-right tiles (which are written last in the Morton curve) were mathematically assigned higher `ix` bounds and were dropped, while top-left tiles were kept. This taught us that uniform LOD culling cannot be driven purely by local tile indices if the data is spatially biased.

## 💡 What We Found Out & Fixed

### 1. Zero-Cost CPU Density Culling
To prevent the CPU from sending useless data to the GPU during zoomed-out states, we intercepted the telemetry pipeline inside the WebWorker (`ArrowWorker.ts`). As the `.feather` files are parsed into zero-copy `Float32Arrays`, the worker scans for `minIx` and `maxIx` bounds and attaches them to the tile metadata. 

If a Quadtree branch has a `minIx` that is mathematically guaranteed to be higher than the camera's current `maxIx` Density limit, the CPU immediately prunes the branch. This stops network requests and GPU buffer uploads before they even start.

### 2. Complete-Level BFS Hardcap
To fix the Tiling Artifacts in the "Valley of Death" while keeping performance stable, we implemented a mathematically precise **Complete-Level BFS Hardcap**.

Instead of randomly aborting at 50 tiles, the `TileManager.ts` engine now waits until it has processed **all sibling tiles** on the current Z-level inside the BFS `currentQueue`. 
Only *after* the entire level is evaluated does it check if the GPU budget is saturated (`visibleTiles >= 50`). If saturated, it breaks the loop and refuses to traverse into the deeper, exponentially more expensive `Z+1` level.

This single algorithmic change guarantees uniform point density across the entire screen (eliminating the tiling gaps) while providing an ironclad upper limit on the vertex geometry (eliminating the FPS stutters).

- Total points on screen dynamically adjust perfectly between ~2-5 Million based on zoom level.
